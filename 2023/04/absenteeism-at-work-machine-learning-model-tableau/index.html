    Absenteeism at work - Machine Learning Model & Tableau

    14 April 2023

    Introduction:

    In this project, I analysed the reasons for excessive absenteeism at work and built a machine learning model in Python to determine the main driver of employee absence. The dataset consisted of 700 observations.

    Employees were encouraged to choose one of the categories for reasons for their absence when reporting their absence from work. The categories are as follows:

    • Reason_1/Group_1: various illnesses,
    • Reason_2/Group_2: pregnancy and childbirth-related,
    • Reason_3/Group_3: poisoning,
    • Reason_4/Group_4: the least serious, such as dental appointments and doctor check-ups.

    Other features also considered in the model to check if they have an impact on the excessive absence:

    • Employee age,
    • Transportation cost to commute to work,
    • Number of children,
    • Pets,
    • BMI,
    • Education level of employee,
    • The average daily workload of an employee,
    • Distance to work,
    • Day of the week.

    I created dummy variables for Reasons for absence as well as Education and prevented any possibility of multicollinearity.

    Data Standardization:

    I standardised the data using the Scikit-Learn StandardScaler, where a CustomScaler class was defined to ensure that the dummy variables were not scaled as well as allow for future deployment of the same code in any different case.

    Training and Testing Data Split:

    I split the data into training and testing datasets with an 80-20 split.

    Model Selection and Classification:

    I used logistic regression from Scikit-Learn LogisticRegression as the model to classify observations into either ‘moderately absent’ or ‘excessively absent’ based on whether the number of hours of absence was below (moderate) or above (excessive) the median. The median was chosen to implicitly balance the data into roughly two halves and minimize the ability of machine learning to focus exclusively on only one of the categories (i.e. 1 or 0 or ‘excessive absence’ and ‘moderate absence’).

    Model Evaluation and Feature Selection:

    The testing accuracy achieved was 77.5%, as checked with the ‘predict’ method from Scikit-Learn as well as calculating it manually. A summary table was created to see the results, with bias and weights as well as the calculation of the odds ratio for each.

    Summary Table

    Based on the training results, I dropped the least explanatory features from the model, such as ‘Month of absence’, ‘Daily average workload’, ‘Distance to work’, and ‘Day of the week’. It was surprising to find that the daily average workload did not explain much excessive absence, as it was commonly thought that the larger the workload, the more stressed and hence absent a particular employee could be.

    Results:

    The biggest explanatory power for excessive absence seemed to have the official reasons. Since Reason_0 was dropped to avoid multicollinearity, I found that there is a higher chance of excessive absence occurring when the official reason for absence is stated by the employee. The model was then tested and its accuracy was 75%.

    Saving the Model:

    The model and scaler were saved using ‘pickle’. A module was created so that the model with preprocessing and scaler could be then re-used on future data.

    Deployment:

    The module was then deployed on a new dataset and the results of the analysis were exported into a .csv. The results of the deployment of the model on new data were then imported into Tableau to visualize some of the findings.

    Findings:

    We can see that the age of an employee does not cause much excessive absence. However, the older the person gets, the more likely they will need to take more absences than younger people. Most of the employees in the new dataset were aged 20-40.

    Age vs Probability of Excessive Absence

    Looking at the 4 categories of reasons for absence, Reason_2 (pregnancy and childbirth-related) was dropped, as there were no observations. We can see that people who were absent due to illness (Reason_1) were the most likely to be excessively absent, while people who were absent due to a medical appointment (Reason_4) were very unlikely to be excessively absent, as medical appointments usually don’t require more than a couple of hours off work. The poisoning had very few observations (Reason_3), which means that employees hardly ever reported poisoning as their reason for work absence.

    Reasons vs Probability of Excessive Absence

    The final graph shows how transportation expenses relate to employee absenteeism, and whether this relationship is influenced by the number of children an employee has.

    From the graph, it’s evident that higher transportation expenses tend to coincide with a higher likelihood of excessive employee absence. Additionally, we can observe that employees with more children generally have higher transportation costs.

    Transportation Expenses & Children vs Probability of Excessive Absence

    Interestingly, employees without children appear to be less affected by transportation expenses. This may be because they have more flexibility in choosing where to live and can prioritise living closer to work. In contrast, employees with children must also consider factors like proximity to schools and childcare facilities.

    Transportation Expenses & No Children vs Probability of Excessive Absence

    For employees with 1-2 children, transportation expenses tend to be higher than those without children, but mostly within the median cost range. However, there are some outliers with particularly high transportation expenses.

    Transportation Expenses & 1-2 Children vs Probability of Excessive Absence

    Conclusion:

    The analysis highlights the importance of official reasons for employee absence. By identifying these reasons as the main driver of excessive absence, employers can take steps to address them, such as offering flexible work arrangements, providing support for employees with health issues, or implementing policies to prevent workplace injuries and accidents.

    Additionally, the analysis suggests that transportation expenses could be a significant factor in employee absenteeism, particularly for those with children. This information could be valuable for employers who may consider offering transportation subsidies or other benefits to help alleviate this burden on their employees.

    Overall, the machine learning model developed in Python provides a useful tool for understanding the factors that contribute to excessive employee absenteeism. By analysing a range of features and identifying the key drivers of absence, the model can help employers to make more informed decisions and take proactive steps to improve employee well-being and productivity.
